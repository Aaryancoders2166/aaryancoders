What Is Coding<\/h2>\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t
\n\t\t\t\t\t

Coding\u00a0 <\/b>is the study of the properties of\u00a0codes\u00a0and their respective fitness for specific applications. Codes are used for\u00a0data compression,\u00a0cryptography,\u00a0error detection and correction,\u00a0data transmission\u00a0and\u00a0data storage. Codes are studied by various scientific disciplines\u2014such as\u00a0information theory,\u00a0electrical engineering,\u00a0mathematics,\u00a0linguistics, and\u00a0computer science\u2014for the purpose of designing efficient and reliable\u00a0data transmission\u00a0methods. This typically involves the removal of redundancy and the correction or detection of errors in the transmitted data.<\/p>

There are four types of coding:<\/sup><\/p>

  1. Data compression\u00a0(or\u00a0source coding<\/i>)<\/li>
  2. Error control\u00a0(or\u00a0channel coding<\/i>)<\/li>
  3. Cryptographic coding<\/li>
  4. Line coding<\/li><\/ol>

    Data compression attempts to remove redundancy from the data from a source in order to transmit it more efficiently. For example,\u00a0ZIP data compression\u00a0makes data files smaller, for purposes such as to reduce Internet traffic. Data compression and error correction may be\u00a0studied in combination.<\/p>

    Error correction\u00a0adds extra data bits to make the transmission of data more robust to disturbances present on the transmission channel. The ordinary user may not be aware of many applications using error correction. A typical\u00a0music compact disc\u00a0(CD) uses the\u00a0Reed-Solomon code\u00a0to correct for scratches and dust. In this application the transmission channel is the CD itself. Cell phones also use coding techniques to correct for the\u00a0fading\u00a0and noise of high frequency radio transmission. Data modems, telephone transmissions, and the\u00a0NASA Deep Space Network\u00a0all employ channel coding techniques to get the bits through, for example the\u00a0turbo code\u00a0and\u00a0LDPC codes.<\/p>\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t<\/section>\n\t\t\t\t

    1.Data compression (or source coding)<\/h2>\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t\t

    In\u00a0signal processing,\u00a0data compression<\/b>,\u00a0source coding<\/b>,<\/sup>\u00a0or\u00a0bit-rate reduction<\/b>\u00a0is the process of encoding\u00a0information\u00a0using fewer\u00a0bits than the original representation.<\/sup>\u00a0Any particular compression is either\u00a0lossy\u00a0or\u00a0lossless. Lossless compression reduces bits by identifying and eliminating\u00a0statistical redundancy. No information is lost in lossless compression. Lossy compression reduces bits by removing unnecessary or less important information.<\/sup>\u00a0Typically, a device that performs data compression is referred to as an encoder, and one that performs the reversal of the process (decompression) as a decoder.<\/p>

    The process of reducing the size of a\u00a0data file\u00a0is often referred to as data compression. In the context of\u00a0data transmission, it is called source coding; encoding done at the source of the data before it is stored or transmitted.<\/sup>\u00a0Source coding should not be confused with\u00a0channel coding, for error detection and correction or\u00a0line coding, the means for mapping data onto a signal.<\/p>

    Compression is useful because it reduces resources required to store and transmit data.\u00a0Computational resources\u00a0are consumed in the compression and decompression processes. Data compression is subject to a\u00a0space\u2013time complexity trade-off. For instance,\u00a0a compression scheme for video\u00a0may require expensive\u00a0hardware\u00a0for the video to be decompressed fast enough to be viewed as it is being decompressed, and the option to decompress the video in full before watching it may be inconvenient or require additional storage. The design of data compression schemes involves trade-offs among various factors, including the degree of compression, the amount of distortion introduced (when using\u00a0lossy data compression), and the computational resources required to compress and decompress the data.<\/sup><\/sup><\/p>\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t<\/section>\n\t\t\t\t

    2.Error control (or channel coding)<\/h2>\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t\t

    All error-detection and correction schemes add some\u00a0redundancy\u00a0(i.e., some extra data) to a message, which receivers can use to check consistency of the delivered message, and to recover data that has been determined to be corrupted. Error-detection and correction schemes can be either\u00a0systematic\u00a0or non-systematic. In a systematic scheme, the transmitter sends the original data, and attaches a fixed number of\u00a0check bits<\/i>\u00a0(or\u00a0parity data<\/i>), which are derived from the data bits by some\u00a0deterministic algorithm. If only error detection is required, a receiver can simply apply the same algorithm to the received data bits and compare its output with the received check bits; if the values do not match, an error has occurred at some point during the transmission. In a system that uses a non-systematic code, the original message is transformed into an encoded message carrying the same information and that has at least as many bits as the original message.<\/p>

    Good error control performance requires the scheme to be selected based on the characteristics of the communication channel. Common\u00a0channel models\u00a0include\u00a0memoryless\u00a0models where errors occur randomly and with a certain probability, and dynamic models where errors occur primarily in\u00a0bursts. Consequently, error-detecting and correcting codes can be generally distinguished between\u00a0random-error-detecting\/correcting<\/i>\u00a0and\u00a0burst-error-detecting\/correcting<\/i>. Some codes can also be suitable for a mixture of random errors and burst errors.<\/p>

    If the channel characteristics cannot be determined, or are highly variable, an error-detection scheme may be combined with a system for retransmissions of erroneous data. This is known as\u00a0automatic repeat request\u00a0(ARQ), and is most notably used in the Internet. An alternate approach for error control is\u00a0hybrid automatic repeat request\u00a0(HARQ), which is a combination of ARQ and error-correction coding.<\/p>\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t<\/section>\n\t\t\t\t

    3.Cryptographic coding<\/h2>\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t\t

    Cryptography<\/b>, or\u00a0cryptology<\/b> \u00a0is the practice and study of techniques for\u00a0secure communication\u00a0in the presence of third parties called\u00a0adversaries.<\/sup>\u00a0More generally, cryptography is about constructing and analyzing\u00a0protocols\u00a0that prevent third parties or the public from reading private messages;<\/sup>\u00a0various aspects in\u00a0information security\u00a0such as data\u00a0confidentiality,\u00a0data integrity,\u00a0authentication, and\u00a0non-repudiation<\/sup>\u00a0are central to modern cryptography. Modern cryptography exists at the intersection of the disciplines of\u00a0mathematics,\u00a0computer science,\u00a0electrical engineering,\u00a0communication science, and\u00a0physics. Applications of cryptography include\u00a0electronic commerce,\u00a0chip-based payment cards,\u00a0digital currencies,\u00a0computer passwords, and\u00a0military communications.<\/p>

    Cryptography prior to the modern age was effectively synonymous with\u00a0encryption<\/i>, converting information from a readable state to unintelligible\u00a0nonsense. The sender of an encrypted message shares the decoding technique only with intended recipients to preclude access from adversaries. The cryptography literature\u00a0often uses the names\u00a0Alice (“A”) for the sender, Bob (“B”) for the intended recipient, and Eve (“eavesdropper”) for the adversary.<\/sup>\u00a0Since the development of\u00a0rotor cipher machines\u00a0in\u00a0World War\u00a0I\u00a0and the advent of\u00a0computers\u00a0in\u00a0World War\u00a0II, cryptography methods have become increasingly complex and its applications more varied.<\/p>

    <\/span>Modern cryptography is heavily based on mathematical theory and computer science practice; cryptographic\u00a0algorithms\u00a0are designed around\u00a0computational hardness assumptions, making such algorithms hard to break in actual practice by any adversary. While it is theoretically possible to break into a well-designed such system, it is infeasible in actual practice to do so. Such schemes, if well designed, are therefore termed “computationally secure”; theoretical advances, e.g., improvements in\u00a0integer factorization\u00a0algorithms, and faster computing technology require these designs to be continually reevaluated, and if necessary, adapted. There exist\u00a0information-theoretically secure\u00a0schemes that provably cannot be broken even with unlimited computing power =\u2014 an example is the\u00a0one-time pad\u00a0\u2014- but these schemes are much more difficult to use in practice than the best theoretically breakable but computationally secure schemes.<\/p>

    The growth of cryptographic technology has raised a number of legal issues in the information age. Cryptography’s potential for use as a tool for\u00a0espionage\u00a0and\u00a0sedition\u00a0has led many governments to classify it as a weapon and to limit or even prohibit its use and export.<\/sup>\u00a0In some jurisdictions where the use of cryptography is legal, laws permit investigators to\u00a0compel the disclosure\u00a0of encryption keys for documents relevant to an investigation.<\/sup><\/sup>\u00a0Cryptography also plays a major role in\u00a0digital rights management\u00a0and\u00a0copyright infringement\u00a0disputes in regard to digital media.<\/sup><\/p>\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t<\/section>\n\t\t\t\t

    4.Line coding<\/h2>\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t
    \n\t\t\t\t\t

    In\u00a0telecommunication, a\u00a0line code<\/b>\u00a0is a pattern of voltage, current, or photons used to represent digital data\u00a0transmitted\u00a0down a\u00a0transmission line. This repertoire of signals is usually called a\u00a0constrained code<\/b>\u00a0in data storage systems. Some signals are more prone to error than others when conveyed over a\u00a0communication channel\u00a0as the physics of the communication or\u00a0storage medium\u00a0constrains the repertoire of signals that can be used reliably.<\/sup><\/p>\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t\t\t\t<\/div>\n\t\t<\/section>\n\t\t\t\t
